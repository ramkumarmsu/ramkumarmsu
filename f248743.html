Overview

This course will explore important infrastructure components of the Internet, their vulnerabilities, and strategies to mitigate vulnerabilities. In particular, cryptographic strategies for securing the Internet infrastructure will be explored.

Topics

The course will begin with an overview of critical (Internet related) infrastructure, and a broad description of why they exist, and how they function. This will be followed by student presentations. Two core infrastructures for the functioning of the Internet are 1) Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) and 2) the Domain Name System (DNS). Often, many systems that support this infrastructure are also seen as core infrastructural components. Some examples include
  1. Root Servers,
  2. Internet Exchange Points (IXPs)
  3. Submarine Cable Systems,
  4. Content Delivery Networks (CDNs),
  5. Certificate Authorities (CAs) and PKI,
  6. Time Synchronization Infrastructure (NTP),
  7. IANA and Regional Internet Registries (RIRs),
  8. Inter-AS routing systems, etc.
Students are required to structure their presentations around such core components. Due to the extensive scope of topics, specific topics that will be addressed in this course will depend on topics chosen by students. Some additional suggested topics (in no particular order) for student presentations are
  1. Dyn DDoS Attack (2016),
  2. DNSpionage Campaign (2018-2019),
  3. Sea Turtle Campaign (2019),
  4. YouTube Pakistan Incident (2008),
  5. China Telecom BGP Hijack (2010),
  6. Amazon Route 53 BGP Hijack (2018),
  7. Google BGP Mishap (2018),
  8. Facebook Global Outage (2021),
  9. October 2002 DDoS Attack,
  10. February 2007 DDoS Attack,
  11. November-December 2015 Reflection/Amplification Attack,
  12. 2016 Mirai Botnet Attacks,
  13. Anycast,
  14. PKI and RPKI,
  15. BGPSec,
  16. DNSSEC
Students are expected to identify and suggest at least 3 suitable technical papers / articles for their presentation (in the order of preference) by September 7. This will be used to begin scheduling of student presentations and discussions for the remainder of the course. Presentation schedule will be posted in canvas as soon as the schedule is finalized. As schedule may change due to various reasons, students are expected to check Canvas announcements regularly. The course will will start with 2-3 introductory lectures from the instructor.

ADD-DROP POLICY

  1. Add/drop without penalty

    A student has through the fifth class day into the semester to add a course and through the tenth class day to drop a course without being assessed a fee or academic penalty.

  2. Drop after the tenth class day through the 30th class day into the semester

    A student who elects to drop a course during this period must receive the approval of his/her adviser, will be assigned a W on his/her academic record, and be assessed a fee. The adviser who permits the drop will specify its effective date.

  3. Drop after the 30th class day into the semester

    A student cannot drop courses after this period except in documented cases of serious illness, extreme hardship, or failure of the instructor to provide significant assessment of his/her performance. A request to drop a course during this period must be approved by the student's adviser and academic dean. The dean who permits the drop will specify its effective date. A student receiving permission to drop will receive a W on his/her academic record and be assessed a fee.

  4. Faculty are expected to provide a student with significant evidence or assessment of his/her class performance within the first six weeks.
